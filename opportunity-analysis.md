# Opportunity Analysis — Hospital Queue Management Platform

**Author:** Karani Njoroge
**Date:** June 2026

---

## Why This Problem Is Worth Solving

### Scale
Kenya has 10,000+ public health facilities. Level 4 and 5 hospitals serving urban populations see 200–800 outpatients daily. The queue problem affects millions of patient visits per year.

### Timing
M-Pesa penetration is above 80% in Kenya. Smartphone ownership is growing rapidly in urban areas. The infrastructure to deliver SMS and app-based queue updates exists today in a way it did not five years ago.

### Market Gap
Existing queue management solutions are built for retail and banking (high-income, tech-forward environments). None are designed for the constraints of public health facilities: intermittent connectivity, mix of feature phones and smartphones, low digital literacy among older patients, and zero ability to charge users directly.

### Strategic Importance
Kenya's Universal Health Coverage (UHC) push creates institutional buyers. County health departments and national programs have budget for technology that demonstrably improves throughput and patient experience metrics.

---

## Who Would Buy This

This is a B2B2C product. The buyer is the health facility administrator or county health department. The user is the patient and the nurse.

Direct-to-consumer monetization is not viable in public health. The business model is institutional licensing.

---

## Size of Opportunity

- ~60 Level 4 and 5 public hospitals in Kenya as initial TAM
- Expanding to Level 3 facilities: ~500 facilities
- Regional expansion: Uganda, Tanzania, Rwanda share identical problems

---

## Why Now

1. Digital health investment in Africa is growing post-COVID
2. Government health digitization programs are creating procurement pathways
3. MPESA-native SMS infrastructure makes low-tech delivery viable
4. No dominant player has captured this specific niche

---

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Facilities lack reliable internet | High | High | SMS-first design, offline capability |
| Low patient digital literacy | Medium | High | USSD fallback, community health worker onboarding |
| Procurement cycles are slow | High | Medium | Pilot with NGO partners first |
| Staff resistance to new system | Medium | High | Co-design process with nurses from day one |
