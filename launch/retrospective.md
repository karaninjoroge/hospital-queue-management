# Retrospective — Hospital Queue Management Platform

**Author:** Karani Njoroge
**Date:** June 2026
**Stage:** Post-Discovery / Pre-Build

---

## What I Got Right

**Starting with research before solutions.** My initial instinct was to jump to features. The research process revealed that the problem was uncertainty, not wait time — a distinction that fundamentally changed the product direction. Without the interviews, I would have built the wrong thing.

**Documenting assumptions explicitly.** Writing out what I assumed to be true (patients want faster service; smartphones are the right channel; the patient is the primary user) before testing them made the invalidations legible and actionable.

**Including staff as equal users.** Early framing focused entirely on patients. Interviews with Nurse Mary and others showed that staff adoption is a prerequisite for any patient benefit. The product without a staff dashboard would have failed.

---

## What I Would Do Differently

**Recruit more diverse patient participants.** All three patients I interviewed were female and between 26–58. I missed the perspective of younger male patients and older rural patients who may have different behaviors and constraints.

**Pressure-test the re-entry feature earlier.** I added the grace window as a solution to a pattern I heard in interviews. I should have gone back to at least two interviewees to test whether a 45-minute window is realistic given facility context before including it in the PRD.

**Quantify the staff time loss more rigorously.** "1–2 hours" was self-reported. A more rigorous approach would have involved observing a morning session and timing queue management activities directly.

---

## Key Lessons

1. The most important PM skill in discovery is resisting the solution and staying with the problem longer than feels comfortable.
2. Non-users (in this case: patients who abandon) are as important to understand as active users.
3. Offline functionality is not a nice-to-have in low-infrastructure contexts — it is the product's foundation.

---

## What This Project Taught Me About PM

This case study confirmed that the job of discovery is to replace assumptions with evidence — not to validate your initial idea. The product that emerged from research is meaningfully different from the product I would have designed without it.
